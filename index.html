Mozambique Scoping Update

Andrew Hobbs

January 2014

What are we doing in Mozambique?

Part of CPI's Production-Protection portfolio (with Brazil and Indonesia).

Supported by Barclays Africa in partnership with Technoserve.

Initial focus on physical, economic, institutional, and social landscape.

Now moving into project mode.

Key Points:

  • Land use drives Mozambique's carbon emissions, climate risk, and low income.
  • Most land is not cultivated.
  • Cultivated land has low productivity.
  • Uncultivated land is deforested for household fuel, especially near cities.
  • The PP Framework will help us understand why.

A brief history and mini-geography

  • Portugal arrived 1498.
  • Revolution began September 1964 (FRELIMO formed 1962).
  • Independence June 25, 1975 (coup in Lisbon in 1974).
  • Civil war begins in 1977.
  • Civil war ends October 4, 1992 (talks began in 1990).
  • First election in 1994.

Mozambican incomes are land-use driven.

Agriculture employs 90% of women and
        70% of men in Mozambique.
It represents 24% of the country's GDP.

Climate risk is serious in Mozambique.

Mozambique is among the world's most at risk countries for droughts and flooding from climate change.

Flooding and droughts are increasing in frequency and severity.

How Mozambique uses land:

Even on cultivated land, productivity is low.

Uncultivated land is deforested for fuel.

Each year, 529 square miles of land are deforested for fuel.
50% of the country remains forested.
The forest is shrinking 0.5-1.5% each year.

Production-Protection

Barriers: Technology & Institutions

  • Less than 10% of farmers use improved seeds in maize, and closer to 3-4% for other crops.
  • Fertilizer is expensive and rarely used.
  • Less than 2% of irrigable land is irrigated.
  • Less than 10% of farmers have access to extension services.

Land rights are difficult to obtain

Only 7 million of estimated 32.4 million uncultivated arable hectares are considered "available."

Barriers: Finance

  • Only 20% of finance is in rural areas, despite the fact that 60% of the population lives there.
  • Only 6% of total lending goes to agriculture.
  • Interest rates for the loans that do exist are as high as 30%.
  • However banks have money - current loan to debt ratio is only 50-55%.

Barriers: Risk Management

  • Smallholder risk assessment is very difficult.
  • Weather affects production and prices.
  • Currency risk can increase the cost of finance.

Barriers: Markets & Infrastructure

  • Energy production is massive, but it doesn't reach Mozambique's population (it goes to South Africa instead).
  • Government aims to extend to grid to reach 15% of the population by 2015 and 20% by 2020.
  • Limited access to electricity means irrigation, cooling, and processing are challenging.
  • FUNAE is running a program to promote rural electrification (and CPI may be involved)
